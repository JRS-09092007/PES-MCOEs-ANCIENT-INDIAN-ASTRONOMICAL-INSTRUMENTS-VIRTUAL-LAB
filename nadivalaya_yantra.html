The Nadivalaya Yantra is an equatorial sundial, a key instrument in classical Indian astronomy found in the Jantar Mantar observatories.

It consists of two massive circular masonry dials facing exactly North and South. The dial faces are parallel to the Earth's equator. A central bronze gnomon points directly to the celestial poles (parallel to the Earth's axis).

Unlike flat horizontal sundials, the Nadivalaya provides highly accurate readings because its geometry perfectly mirrors the Earth's relationship with the Sun.

Instrument Tilt

To align the dials with the celestial equator, the dial assembly is tilted away from the zenith by an angle exactly equal to the local Latitude (Φ). You can adjust this tilt in the Simulation tab.

Solar Declination (δ)

The active face depends on the season. The declination is calculated as:
δ ≈ 23.45° × sin[ (360/365) × (d - 81) ]

Face Activation

  • If δ > 0 (Mar 21 - Sep 23): Sun is above the celestial equator. The North Face casts a shadow.
  • If δ < 0 (Sep 23 - Mar 21): Sun is below the celestial equator. The South Face casts a shadow.

Scale Layout

A true equatorial dial has 24 hours equally spaced at 15° intervals (360° / 24). Because the instrument leans back towards the pole, 12 Noon is positioned at the lowest physical point of the circle.

Shadow Movement

Looking at the North Face, the sun moves from East to West across the southern sky, causing the shadow to travel Clockwise.

Looking at the South Face, the shadow travels Counter-Clockwise.
